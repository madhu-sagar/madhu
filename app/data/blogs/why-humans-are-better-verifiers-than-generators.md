# Judgement Is the Most Valuable Skill Today

*This is a rewrite by Claude of an earlier blog post, [Humans are NP Machines](/blog/humans-are-np-machines). The original explored the idea that humans verify far better than they generate. Now that AI has made generation cheap, the argument has sharpened: there is no skill more valuable today than judgement — and downstream from it, using judgement to make better decisions.*

---

You hear a melody for the first time and something clicks — you know instantly that it's beautiful. You couldn't have composed it yourself, not in a hundred years, but you recognised its beauty within seconds. This asymmetry — effortless recognition paired with near-impossible generation — is one of the most fundamental features of human cognition, and it shows up everywhere.

You watch a dancer move and you feel the difference between competence and mastery in your bones. You look at a painting and something in you responds before you've had time to think. You taste a dish and know immediately whether the person who cooked it understood what they were doing. In each case, the judgment is fast, confident, and largely automatic. But ask yourself: could you have produced any of these things? Almost certainly not without years of dedicated practice, and even then, probably not at that level.

We are, it turns out, extraordinary judges and mediocre creators.

## The Verification-Generation Gap

There's a concept in computer science that mirrors this asymmetry surprisingly well. In complexity theory, there's a class of problems called NP — problems where checking whether a given solution is correct can be done efficiently, even if finding that solution from scratch is extraordinarily hard. Think of a massive jigsaw puzzle: verifying that someone has completed it correctly is trivial — you just look at it. But assembling it yourself, piece by piece, is a different ordeal entirely.

Human cognition seems to operate along similar lines. We are equipped with powerful verification machinery — pattern recognition, aesthetic intuition, emotional resonance — that lets us evaluate outputs almost instantly. But the generation of those outputs requires something else entirely: technique, knowledge, practice, and often a kind of creative search through a vast space of possibilities that our conscious minds aren't built to do efficiently.

This isn't a perfect formal analogy. Human cognition doesn't run on Turing machines, and there's no clean way to define "polynomial time" for the experience of being moved by a piece of music. But the structural shape is the same: checking is cheap, producing is expensive.

## Where This Shows Up

The gap is visible nearly everywhere once you start looking for it.

In mathematics, understanding and verifying a proof is dramatically easier than discovering one. A student can follow Euler's proof that there are infinitely many primes and confirm each step's validity in minutes. Euler had to see something no one had seen before.

In cooking, you can taste a dish and tell whether the spice balance is right, whether the texture works, whether something is missing. Reproducing that dish — let alone inventing it — demands a completely different kind of knowledge. Your palate is a verification engine. Your hands in the kitchen are the generator, and they need years of training.

In hiring and talent recognition, most experienced managers can quickly identify a strong candidate during an interview. But being that candidate — having built the skills, judgment, and track record — took the candidate years of compounding effort.

Even in conversation, you can recognise wit instantly. Producing it on demand is another matter entirely.

## How We Compensate: Imitation and Apprenticeship

If humans are fundamentally better at recognising quality than producing it, how does quality get produced at all? The answer, I think, lies in one of our oldest social technologies: imitation.

Solomon Asch demonstrated that humans are wired for social consensus — we look to others to calibrate our behaviour. But this tendency, often framed as a weakness, is also a powerful learning mechanism. We watch someone do something well, we verify internally that it is indeed good (using our cheap verification ability), and then we attempt to copy it. The cycle of observe, verify, imitate is how skills propagate through cultures.

This is why nearly every civilisation has developed the institution of the master and the apprentice. The guru, the sensei, the ustād, the mentor — these are people who have already solved the generation problem in their domain. The apprentice's job is not to rediscover everything from scratch. It is to use their natural verification ability to recognise what the master does well, and then to close the gap between recognition and reproduction through deliberate practice.

Tradition, in this framing, is not mere conservatism. It is an efficient strategy for a species that verifies cheaply but generates expensively. Why search the entire solution space yourself when someone has already found a good answer and you can confirm it's good just by looking at it?

## Taste as a Genuine Skill

This reframing has an implication that I find quietly important: it elevates the role of the verifier.

We tend to treat taste, curation, and judgment as lesser skills — secondary to the "real" work of creation. But if verification is genuinely a distinct and valuable cognitive capacity, then the critic, the curator, the editor, and the talent scout are not parasitic on creators. They are performing a function that is both cognitively real and economically essential. They are the NP verifiers in a world that needs someone to separate signal from noise.

The person who can walk through a gallery and tell you which three paintings will endure is doing something non-trivial, even if they cannot paint. The reader who can tell you exactly why a sentence doesn't work is contributing to the craft, even if they have never published a novel.

Generation is rare and hard. Verification is common and fast. But a world without good verifiers would drown in mediocre output with no way to find the gold. Both capacities matter. It's just that we've been undervaluing one of them for a very long time.
